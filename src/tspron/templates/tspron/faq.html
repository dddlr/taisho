What are the stuff in parentheses/round brackets?

In parentheses is the word category (or categories) of a particular word. The word categories used on this website are listed below; they work virtually the same way as in Cantonese (and to a lesser but still considerable extent, Mandarin), and so only the differences will be described here.

These explanations of word categories are partially based on that of the fantastic ABC Chinese-English Dictionary.

  • 名 is short for 名詞, “noun”. This includes time words (e.g. 今日 geom3 meot5, “today”) and proper nouns (騰訊 hang4 hleon3, “Tencent”), but excludes pronouns (你 ni3, “you (singular)”)
  • 代 is short for 代詞, “pronoun”.
  • 形 is short for 形容詞, “adjective” (though also called “stative verb”). Although its function is similar to adjectives in English, they behave a lot like verbs. For example, they have the sense of “to be” already embedded in them (e.g. 靚 liang3 means “to be beautiful” and not just “beautiful”). A more in-depth explanation about Chinese “adjectives” vs. verbs (in particular, stative verbs) can be found here.
  • 代
  • 助
  • 動 is short for 動詞, “verb”. Note that this category doesn’t include verbs that are actually verb-object constructions, which are instead labeled 動賓 (see below).
  • 動賓 (literally “verb object”) is short for 動賓結構, “verb-object construction”, and is composed of a single-syllable verb with a single-syllable noun. A lot of Chinese verbs are actually verb-object constructions. These include 吃飯 (hiak3 fan6, “to eat a meal”) and 偷稅 (heu3 sui3, “to evade taxes”). Chinese Grammar Wiki calls these separable verbs and explains how they work here.
  • 成語
